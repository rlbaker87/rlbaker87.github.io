Active Shooter Response Training

A full redesign moved the active shooter module from passive policy review to scenario-driven practice that builds real decision-making under pressure.

30K+ Team Members — Deployment Target
4 Scenario Decision Points
21 Content Items Preserved via Crosswalk
2026 Scheduled Deployment Cycle

The Challenge

The prior compliance training was built on a slide-and-read model. Learning objectives used vague verbs — understand, review, determine — that could not be observed, measured, or assessed. The active shooter content in particular relied on definition recall and policy recitation, which does not prepare clinical or non-clinical team members to act when a threat is immediate, unpredictable, and fast-moving.

The redesign had to serve 30,000+ team members across widely different roles and work environments, with no assumption that the learner works in a clinical setting.

Solution

  • Rewrote all four course learning objectives using action-based verbs tied to observable performance — Recognize, Apply, Assess, Select — reframing what the course required of learners from passive reading to decision-making in realistic situations.
  • Redesigned the active shooter module around a scenario framework grounded in a nationally aligned three-option response model: Run, Hide, Fight. The module opens with a brief explainer on active shooter incident characteristics specific to healthcare settings, then moves directly into practice.
  • Built a four-step custom HTML interaction structured as a single continuous event: active threat in the building, exit blocked, shooter attempting entry, and law enforcement arriving on scene. Each step places the learner at a decision point and holds all feedback until every choice is made, then delivers a structured debrief covering what was done right, what could increase risk, and why it matters.
  • Appended a fifth informational screen covering Stop the Bleed kit awareness, connecting the post-threat phase to a concrete post-incident action expectation without adding a separate course block.
  • Conducted a content crosswalk of the prior course identifying 21 content items at risk of being lost in the redesign — including active shooter incident characteristics, 911 reporting guidance, and healthcare-specific location data. All critical items were preserved through scenario feedback and performance support resources rather than passive content blocks.
  • Integrated the active shooter content with Infant Abduction as a paired high-risk events module, reusing the same custom interaction architecture via content swap — giving both scenarios a consistent design pattern and reducing future maintenance overhead.
  • Framed the full course under a Just Culture lens in the intro video script, explicitly connecting awareness, accountability, and coordinated response as the design through-line across all four modules.
Design Decision

Feedback is withheld until all four decisions are made. This mirrors real conditions — you don't get corrective feedback mid-event. The debrief lands harder when the learner sees the full pattern of their choices together, not one answer at a time.
